We are a grassroots organized business cooperative located in Jimaní, a small town near the Haitian/Dominican border. COTEDEMI has been in operation since February of 2013  to build job opportunities, entrepreneurship and to provide financial services to its members. The majority of our mebers are refugee women affected by the 2010 Earthquake in Haiti. We are creating a better future for ourselves and our families by working together and with trusted partners. We provide culturally and linguistically sensitive programs that focus on business development and financial savings.   As an Open Source project, we share all of our history and information on a publically shared repository where we can edit, update and manage group documents with our partners. https://github.com/COTEDEMI EndFragment

What do you actually do? - Supervise weekly savings circles that allow women lacking access to formal banking to save and borrow money with people they trus t        (Our weekly workshops focus on business growth, marketing, savings for legal documentation, healthcare and education) - Assist business owners to secure proper documentation and pay business taxes to the council of the town of Jimaní - Provide small loans from $50­-500 for capable entrepreneurs who wish to build their business or create new ones Who are your partners?   In conjunction with Banco ADOPEM's  "community banking” product, we are assisting undocumented migrants a formal way to save, borrow and have access to workshops in the areas of financial literacy, entrepreneurship, health and education. We are also working with programers and software developers in both the Dominican Republic and the United States.  How do you engage women? Local workshops, cultural events and group activities, financial literacy training, etc.  -hackathons -entrepreneurial challenges  What are the issues with documentation? Legal status is a powerful element that provide individuals with the ability to travel safely and earn more money, thereby lowering rates of poverty. Unlike the United States, the DR does not grant citizenship to children who are born to undocumented parents. Currently hundreds of thousands of individuals of Haitian descent are denied access to affordable health services, basic legal protections and the right to vote. In Jimaní all children are allowed to attend primary school however once they reach middle school, they are required to provide proper documentation. If documentation is not available their education ends. Our program helps families whose children are dependent on documentation to attend school. We are serving 95 women and 130 of their dependents and believe that our model provides families a trusted place to save for this documentation. We have the potential of assisting 1,000 families to become integrated, visible and active members of Dominican society.

As a result of the 2010 Earthquake, an estimated 200,000 Haitians fled to the DR in search of medical assistance, employment and relief from post­disaster conditions. The Haitian women with whom we work are oftentimes subjected to extortion and physical and sexual violence, and earn an average of $2.50/day. Without proper legal documentation, they are denied employment and relegated to a never­ending cycle of poverty. We assist women to adjust their status by securing proper documentation.
